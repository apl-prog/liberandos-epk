LIBERANDOS acts as a sonic bridge between worlds. The duo (Adam Lindquist and Ben Mabry) reimagines the hypnotic rhythms of North Africa and Indian chant through a Southern roots lens. Layered with hand percussion and intricate strings, these physical, circular grooves provide the foundation for a soulful vocal delivery that explores a modern, mythic lyricism. The songs unfold as a slow-burning dialogue—often leading to intense, almost psychedelic builds - resulting in a sound that feels both ancient and unresolved.

Abandoning the drum kit entirely, Liberandos builds its music from hand percussion, acoustic guitars, bass, and DIY orchestras of strings and winds. Drawing on pentatonic and Eastern-influenced approaches, the arrangements often leave harmonic space intentionally open, giving the songs a ritual, trance-like pull.

Ben’s vocals and lyrics move through these structures intuitively, shaped by a Southern Pentecostal upbringing, apocalyptic imagery, and the grit of ’90s alternative rock. His melodies complete the harmonic picture, balancing restraint and intensity, while the lyrics draw from inherited spiritual language refracted through a present-day cultural weight.

The duo often imagines each song as a physical place: a village gathering where everyone brings an instrument, a ceremony unfolding inside a pyramid, or a journey through a walled garden. These settings guide the arrangements and give the music a communal, cinematic quality.

Mabry (Beta Radio) and Lindquist (River Lume) step outside their respective projects with Liberandos to explore a shared language — one tuned to a strange, bleeding frequency where ancient rhythm and Southern song meet.

Blood Orange

Debut Single — March 20, 2026

Download AIFF SoundCloud Bandcamp

Blood Orange is built on a cycling guitar and bass motif. The arrangement adds layers of pulsing, intricate strings, providing a cinematic scale while keeping the track’s organic grit. The commanding vocal performance maintains a constant tension throughout, finally leading into a heavy, psychedelic climax.

Thematically, the song emerged from what singer and lyricist Ben describes as “scattered digital ephemera” — odd phrases and images saved for later. The track contemplates the moment of realization in a doomed relationship; that point where sheer will is exhausted and something supernatural breaks through. In the song’s “garden,” the act of eating the blood orange signifies a reversal of the Eden narrative: it is not a fall from grace, but an awakening into clarity and freedom.
