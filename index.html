Liberandos — EPK

LIBERANDOS is a sonic bridge between two worlds. At its core, the duo (Adam Lindquist and Ben Mabry) engages in a unique act of musical translation: taking the hypnotic, rhythmic traditions of North Africa and Indian chant and reimagining them through their Southern roots background.

Abandoning the drum kit entirely, Adam constructs dense, trance-like foundations from hand percussion, droning guitars, and DIY orchestras (violins, clarinets), often drawing on pentatonic and Eastern-inspired scales that leave harmonic space open and unresolved. The result is a music that feels circular and ritualistic.

Ben’s vocals and lyrics move through these structures intuitively, shaped by a Southern Pentecostal upbringing, the looming imagery of Armageddon, and the grit of ’90s alternative rock. His melodies often complete the harmonic picture, filling in emotional color, with a push-and-pull of intensity. Lyrically, the songs sit at the intersection of inherited spiritual language and a present-day cultural heaviness — music that acknowledges dread without dramatizing it.

The duo often imagines each song as a physical place: a village gathering where everyone brings an instrument, a ceremony unfolding inside a pyramid, or a journey through a walled garden. Those imagined settings guide the arrangements, often giving the music a collective, almost communal feeling.

Ben Mabry is also one half of the indie-folk duo Beta Radio, while Adam Lindquist releases solo work under the name River Lume. With Liberandos, the two step outside their other projects to explore a shared language — one tuned to a strange, bleeding frequency where ancient rhythm and Southern song meet.

Fans of Tinariwen, Khruangbin, or early Beirut — especially those drawn to roots music that borrows freely across traditions — may find a natural entry point.
